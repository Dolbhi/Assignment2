Historical bushfires in Australia

Bushfires in Australia are not a new development, Australia's dry climate means it contains some of the most fire-prone regions in the world. Here bushfires have been a widespread and regular occurance for millions of year and plays a key role in shaping the environment, with many plants evolving to take advantage of land cleared by fires.

However, in the modern day, out of control bushfires can cause great damage to property and person, and with climate change giving rise to more extreme weather, the delicate balance of burning and regrowth may be in danger of tipping.

Causes and factors

Many fires have man-made causes such as electrical arcing, cigerettes or sparks from machinary. However, in recent times, most major fires have started from dry lightning igniting fires in remote regions.

On the other hand, the intensity and severity of fires depends on how much dry fuel is avaliable to it. While bushfires can occur all year round, Fires then to be more ferocious in hotter drier conditions thus making them more widespread in the summer or in what is typically refered to as "bushfire season". In truth, there is no formal definition for a bushfire season shared across the whole of Australia and the exact period of greatest fire activity varies from region to region.

Typically, the colloquial use of "bushfire season" applies mainly to southern and eastern Australia when uncontrolled fires start occuring as early as June and may continue to occur into April.

Impact of bushfires

As climate change brings about hotter and more extreme weather, bushfires in recent years have become more intense and frequent. In addition to the direct damages to life and property, the smoke released by bushfires can adversely affect the respiratory system of infants. This in turn results has a significant economic impact in the funds spent on reconstruction, firefighting and healthcare.

Wildfires also directly kill fauna and flora and destroy their habitats, posing significant risk to hundreds of already endangered species. Even for animals that are capable of outrunning or burrowing to hide from the blaze tend to suffer high numbers of causalties when doing so. Additionally, forest fires release a large amount of CO2, far more than can be reabsorbed by the forest and fires become hotter and more common.

1938-39: Black Friday

Following years of drought and a series of extreme heatwaves, the 1939 bushfire season was one of the worst on record. Multiple fires that had been burning throughout December linked together on January Friday 13 in the third deadliest fire on record, totalling 71 deaths and 650 houses destroyed.

As a response to the devestating blaze, recommendations were brought forward to improve forest and fire management resulting in planned burning becoming offical practice.


1966-67: Black Tuesday

In the summer of 1967, Tasmania suffered its most destructive fire season, and Australia's fourth most deadly on record. A verdant spring had added higher than usual fuel to the state's forest floors, and strong northerly winds and high temperatures drove at least 80 different fires across the south-east, burning to within 2 kilometres of the centre of Hobart. The infernos killed 62 people and destroyed almost 1,300 homes.


1980-83: Ash Wednesday

Another series of severe droughts and extreme weather over serveral years led to about 180 fires on 16 February 1983, in an event known as the Ash Wednesday bushfires caused widespread destruction across parts of South Australia and Victoria, burning an area of 1.51 million of bushland and killing over 75 people.


2003: Canberra bushfires

From 18-22 January 2003, almost 70% of the Australian Capital Territory's pastures, pine plantations, and nature parks were severely damaged, and most of the Mount Stromlo Observatory was destroyed. Fires entered the suburbs of Canberra on 18 January 2003 and, resulting in 4 deaths, 490 injuries and 470 homes destroyed or damaged.

2019-20: The Hottest Year

2019 had been the hottest record year for Australia, with the bushfire season starting in June 2019. This caused massive damage throughout the country, with fires in each state and territory. The east coast experienced widespread destruction from mega-blazes, such as the Currowan bushfire, which was just one of many catastrophic bushfires during the 2019-2020 season. In terms of the area of land burnt, wildlife deaths, and damage to the environment, including the burning of remnant rainforest, the 2019-20 fires were the worst in Australian history.
